# Review: Policy, product, and actionability

## Scope

Check regulatory accuracy, design proposals, synthesis levers, feasibility, and whether recommendations are grounded.

## Overall assessment

- **Strengths**
  - The series is unusually disciplined about regulatory humility: articles repeatedly state that regulation is a "floor," that compliance is not sufficient, and that no single actor can solve the attention problem. This avoids the common trap of overpromising what law can do.
  - Design proposals (chronological feeds, friction against virality, user-chosen algorithms, age-appropriate defaults) are framed as exploratory directions with explicit boundaries, not as utopian blueprints or one-size-fits-all mandates.
  - The levers map in `a-map-of-levers` distributes responsibility across six layers and explicitly argues that single-layer interventions will be absorbed or gamed, which is a sensible anti-overpromise framing.
  - Counterevidence and caveat language is present in the artifacts, and most articles distinguish illegal-content regulation from design/business-model accountability.

- **Concerns**
  - A few statutory names and implementation statuses are inaccurate or outdated for a July 2026 publication date, especially the "Online Gaming (Regulation) Act, 2025" misnomer and the claim that the DPDP Act's Data Protection Board "had not been constituted" by mid-2026.
  - The Kerala High Court's December 2024 internal staff memorandum is described in several articles as a broader "ban on personal phones during work hours," which overstates its scope.
  - Some articles pair India's IT Rules 2021 with the EU Digital Services Act as comparable "accountability" or "transparency/user-control" instruments, which is misleading because the IT Rules are primarily about content takedown and grievance redressal, not systemic-risk transparency.
  - A couple of time-bound claims have passed their deadlines (e.g., NASSCOM's "$450–500 billion by 2025" estimate) and should be reframed as benchmarks rather than forward-looking forecasts.

- **Verdict:** ready with minor fixes

## Cross-cutting findings

1. **Kerala High Court scope:** The December 2024 order was an internal office-hours memorandum for court staff, not a general ban on personal phone use during work hours. It appears in `a-map-of-levers:103`, `engagement-is-a-design-choice:89`, and `the-engagement-gap-productivity-india:92` (sources list) and is consistently overstated.
2. **Statute-name accuracy:** The "Online Gaming (Regulation) Act, 2025" appears in `a-map-of-levers:99` and `a-map-of-levers:121` and in the agent brief. The correct short name is the Promotion and Regulation of Online Gaming Act, 2025.
3. **DPDP implementation status:** Multiple articles still treat the DPDP Rules as "draft" or the Data Protection Board as "pending/not constituted." The DPDP Rules, 2025 were notified on 13 November 2025 and the Board became operational; articles published in July 2026 should reflect this.
4. **IT Rules vs. EU DSA pairing:** Articles occasionally present India's IT Rules 2021 and the EU DSA as comparable instruments for platform-design accountability. The IT Rules are primarily content/grievance/traceability rules, while the DSA imposes systemic-risk, algorithmic-transparency, and audit obligations. The distinction is correctly drawn in `regulation-as-a-floor` but blurred elsewhere.
5. **Time-bound economic claims:** NASSCOM's "$450–500 billion by 2025" estimate in `the-generational-bet:35` has passed its horizon date. It should be reframed as a prior forecast or benchmark.

## Questions for the author

1. **DPDP Rules status:** Should the series treat the DPDP Rules, 2025 as notified and in phased implementation, or is there a deliberate editorial choice to keep calling them "draft"? The current wording conflicts with the November 2025 notification.
2. **Kerala HC reference:** Is the intent to cite the Kerala High Court's internal staff memorandum as a symbolic signal of institutional concern, or as a legal precedent? If the former, the wording should be narrowed.
3. **IT Rules 2026 update:** In `designing-for-substance`, does "updated in 2026" refer to the deepfake-labelling amendment (notified February 2026) or the broader draft Second Amendment (circulated March 2026)? Clarifying this avoids implying a finalized amendment where only a draft may exist.
4. **Online Gaming Act citation:** Does the Economic Survey 2025–26 actually refer to the statute by the short name "Online Gaming (Regulation) Act," or is the series' short name an invention? If the latter, it should be corrected to the official short title.
