# Expanded Plan: Attention, Substance, and the AI Moment

## Goal

Transform the four published seed articles into a deep, self-contained research series that:
- Keeps every article readable on its own.
- Uses data visualizations and charts to make the evidence concrete.
- Never uses internal "Lane A/B/C/D" labels; cross-references use article titles and links.
- Covers the problem, the AI opportunity cost, individual practice, and systemic design in enough depth that a reader does not need to look elsewhere.
- Is willing to run to 40+ articles if that is what the material demands.

## Series architecture

The series is organized into six arcs. Each arc is a coherent thread; each article is a self-contained chapter. Articles link forward and backward using titles, not arc labels.

### Arc 1 — The Diagnosis (10–12 articles)
The empirical picture of how attention is extracted in India and similar mobile-first countries.

## Visualization strategy

### Chart types to use
- **Bar charts** for comparisons (platform share, time use, engagement, income distribution).
- **Line charts** for trends (attention span over time, Tele-MANAS calls, screen-time growth).
- **Stacked bars / treemaps** for composition (daily online time by activity, ad revenue by platform).
- **Timelines** for historical and regulatory arcs.
- **Flow diagrams** for engagement loops, AI-assisted workflows, and platform incentives.
- **Network/edge diagrams** for content amplification, federation, and accountability ecosystems.
- **Before/after conceptual diagrams** for public spaces, family tables, and design alternatives.
- **Heatmaps** for observational data (phone use by location/time).
- **Lorenz-curve-style charts** for inequality (creator income, attention concentration).
- **Radar charts** for comparing alternative metrics.

### Implementation options
1. **Mermaid diagrams** for flowcharts, timelines, and simple bar charts. Site already depends on Mermaid.  
   Pros: version-controlled, text-based, no external image files.  
   Cons: limited styling, complex charts are verbose.
2. **Python/matplotlib or Plotly** for data-heavy charts, exported as SVG/PNG.  
   Pros: precise, publication-quality, can handle real data.  
   Cons: requires regenerating images when data changes; needs asset pipeline.
3. **Astro component** for interactive charts if the site is upgraded.  
   Pros: interactive, accessible.  
   Cons: more engineering; may not fit current static build.

**Recommendation:** Use Mermaid for conceptual diagrams and Python-generated SVGs for data charts. Store generated charts in each article's workspace or a shared `public/images/articles/` directory.
