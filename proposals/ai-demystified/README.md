# AI, De-Mystified

## Summary

`AI, De-Mystified` is a proposed Aura Knowledge article series for readers who keep encountering new AI terms and want to understand them without being pulled into hype, vendor language, or academic fog.

The series explains one concept at a time using plain language, relevant analogies, practical examples, benefits, limitations, and links to deeper academic ideas. It should help readers move from "I have heard this buzzword" to "I know what problem this solves, what older idea it resembles, where it helps, and where the term is being over-sold."

## Series Promise

Each article should help the reader answer:

- What does this term mean in plain English?
- What older idea is it similar to?
- What is actually new because of large language models?
- Where does it help in real practice?
- Where does it fail or get exaggerated?
- What academic concepts sit behind it?
- How could I use this idea in my own learning, writing, coding, product work, or agent workflows?

## Reader

The primary reader is a curious builder, student, creator, or knowledge worker who uses AI tools but does not want to memorize jargon or treat every new term as a breakthrough.

This reader is comfortable with analogies and practical examples. They may later want academic depth, but the first job of each article is clarity.

## Recommended Positioning

Publish this as a series inside Aura Knowledge first, rather than as a separate website.

The series belongs in Aura Knowledge because it is not only about AI tools. It is about knowledge literacy: how to understand new terms, compare them with existing concepts, identify what is reusable, and separate substance from branding.

If the series gains a stable audience after several articles, it can later be extracted into a dedicated microsite, index page, or newsletter archive.

## Naming

Recommended public title:

`AI, De-Mystified`

Recurring section:

`The De-Hype Check`

This keeps the educational tone first while still preserving the skeptical lens.

## Article Pattern

Each article should follow a simple repeatable shape:

1. Plain English Meaning
2. Existing Concept It Resembles
3. What Is Actually New?
4. How It Works In Practice
5. Where It Helps
6. Where It Fails
7. Academic Connections
8. Practical Checklist
9. The De-Hype Check
10. Open Questions

The structure should stay light. Articles should not feel like taxonomies or academic survey papers unless a later technical companion is needed.

## First Article

Recommended first article:

`Loops vs Goals: The Difference Between Repetition and Direction in AI Agents`

Core thesis:

Goals define what an AI system is trying to achieve. Loops define how it repeatedly makes progress. Long-running AI sessions need both, plus memory, context management, evaluation, and stopping rules.

Suggested explanation:

```text
Goal = destination
Loop = engine
Memory = what should survive
Context = what the model needs right now
Evaluation = how we know the work is improving
```

This article should explain why loops are useful for long-running sessions, but only when governed by clear goals, exit conditions, progress checks, and human escalation when stuck.

## Learning Cadence

The series should be written for regular study, not all-at-once consumption.

Recommended cadence for each concept:

1. Learn the concept briefly.
2. Compare it with older concepts.
3. Discuss practical value and failure modes.
4. Draft a shareable article.
5. Refine with examples, sources, and open questions.

## Anti-Hype Lens

Every article should ask:

- What problem does this solve?
- What is the old name for this idea?
- What is genuinely different because of LLMs?
- What evidence shows it works?
- What are the costs or tradeoffs?
- What breaks when used at scale?
- Who benefits from making this sound more complicated than it is?

## Scope Rules

- Prefer one concept per article.
- Prefer analogies over dense terminology, but explain where the analogy breaks.
- Include practical examples from learning, writing, coding, product work, and agent workflows.
- Avoid turning early articles into product comparisons.
- Use public sources only.
- Mark fast-changing product behavior as time-sensitive.
- Keep academic connections as optional depth, not required background.
