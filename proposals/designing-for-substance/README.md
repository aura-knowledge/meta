# Designing for Substance

## Opening analogy

Before food safety laws, a factory could sell whatever fit in a can. Before tobacco warnings, a company could market to children. Before seatbelts, carmakers could claim that safety was the driver's private responsibility. In each case, the design of a product was treated as a private business decision until the public costs became too large to ignore. Then the frame shifted: some design choices are not merely commercial; they are civic.

We are at a similar shift with attention-extracting platforms. The question is no longer whether they are legal or popular. It is whether their default designs are compatible with a healthy society.

## Thesis

The attention economy is not inevitable. It is the product of specific design choices—engagement metrics, personalized recommendations, infinite scroll, and ad-based business models—that could be redesigned. Building a substance-friendly internet requires new incentives, new metrics, and new accountability structures at the platform, regulatory, and civic levels.

## Why this article now

Lanes A, B, and C have shown the diagnosis, the stakes, and what individuals can do. But individual virtue cannot outrun systemic design. If the easiest path is still extraction, only a minority will resist it. This article asks: what would it take to make substance the easier path?

## What the reader will understand

After reading, the reader should be able to name three design or policy levers that could shift platform incentives toward substance and recognize that product alternatives are possible.

## What this article is not

- It is not a business plan for a new platform.
- It is not a regulatory handbook.
- It is not a utopian blueprint. It is an exploration of plausible directions.

## Core claims

1. **Engagement is a design choice, not a law of nature.** Platforms optimize for it because their business models reward it; other metrics are possible.
2. **Alternative metrics exist.** Time well spent, creator sustainability, learning outcomes, civic value, and long-term user flourishing can be measured and rewarded.
3. **Accountability can come from multiple directions.** Regulation (transparency, risk assessments, age-appropriate design), public pressure, and user migration to better-designed platforms all create leverage.
4. **Substance-friendly design is a category, not one app.** It includes chronological feeds, user-chosen algorithms, expert curation, reputation systems, and friction against virality.
5. **The product alternative starts with a different question.** Instead of "How do we maximize time on platform?" it asks "How do we help users leave better than they arrived?"

## Life-phase threads

- **Children:** Age-appropriate design means no infinite scroll, no autoplay, no dark patterns, and no algorithmic recommendations before a certain age.
- **Students:** Educational and civic content gets algorithmic priority; entertainment is available but not the default.
- **Workers:** Focus-protective defaults—batch notifications, no infinite feeds during work hours, clear session boundaries.
- **Elderly:** Safety and verification are prioritized; sharing is slowed by a gentle friction that makes misinformation harder to spread.

## Historical analogies

- **Tobacco regulation:** A product once marketed as freedom was later understood as a public-health design problem. Warning labels, marketing limits, and youth restrictions did not end smoking, but they shifted the default.
- **Food safety standards:** Before regulation, producers had incentives to cut corners. Public standards changed what "normal" production looked like.
- **The shift from clickbait to subscription journalism:** When readers paid directly, the metric shifted from clicks to trust.
- **Open-source software:** A different incentive structure—reputation, utility, community—produced durable, inspectable tools without engagement optimization.
