# Agent Brief: The Birth of AI

## Purpose

Prepare a future writer to draft an accessible article on Dartmouth, symbolic
AI, early systems, and early optimism without turning the story into an origin
myth.

## Audience

- General readers who know AI is important but do not know its history.
- Students and builders who need a careful bridge from early symbolic AI to
  later AI debates.
- Agents that need structured claim boundaries and source IDs.

## Use This Thesis

AI's "birth" is best treated as a naming and consolidation moment. Dartmouth
made a field legible, fundable, and discussable, while early symbolic systems
showed that computers could perform some formal activities associated with
intelligence. The lasting lesson is not that intelligence was solved in the
1950s, but that researchers learned how difficult it was to turn intelligence
into symbols, rules, search, and programs.

## Claims to Make Carefully

- Dartmouth was a field-forming event, not the first moment anyone imagined or
  attempted machine intelligence.
- Symbolic AI was central, but not the entire early field.
- Early systems were successful in constrained domains.
- Early optimism was understandable from the period's vantage point.
- Later disappointment should be explained as evidence of problem difficulty,
  not as evidence that early researchers were naive.

## Claims to Avoid

- "AI began at Dartmouth" without an immediate caveat.
- "Logic Theorist understood mathematics."
- "GPS was a universal problem solver" without explaining its constraints.
- "Lisp caused AI" or "Dartmouth caused modern generative AI."
- Modern product comparisons as evidence.
- Private or proprietary anecdotes.

## Suggested Opening

Start with a scene in which a field receives a name before it has solved its
central problem. The tension is useful: a group of researchers could see enough
from logic, computers, and early programs to believe the project was possible,
but they did not yet know how much of intelligence would resist clean
formalization.

## Suggested Ending

End by handing off to the next article on AI winters and expert systems. The
early field created durable tools and questions, but its limits made future
cycles of promise and disappointment almost inevitable.

- How much space should the finished article give to neural and cybernetic

- Should the main article include funding dollar amounts, or leave funding
  details to the technical companion?
- Which visual style best fits the series: timeline-first, causal graph, or a
  "promise versus limit" table?
