# Before Machines: Calculation, Automata, and the Dream of Mechanical Reason

Status: `draft-work-package`

Issue: `aura-knowledge/meta#6`

Series: `The Long Human Road to AI`

Source canon status: provisional pending issue `#5`.

## Purpose

This work package prepares a public article about the pre-electronic roots of
computing and AI. It is not final article prose. It gives a future writer the
scope, claims, source boundaries, analogy limits, and technical companion notes
needed to draft a readable article without turning old tools into fake
"ancient AI" examples.

## Thesis

Before electronic computers existed, people already used tools, tables,
procedures, symbolic systems, and self-moving mechanisms to extend calculation
and imagine mechanical action. Those developments make later computers and AI
more understandable, but they should be presented as context, not as primitive
computers or artificial intelligence.

## Reader Promise

A curious reader should come away with four ideas:

1. Calculation was first a human activity and often a form of organized labor.
2. Calculation aids mattered because they moved fragile mental steps into
   shared, inspectable, repeatable forms.
3. Automata and mechanical calculators showed that parts of action or
   calculation could be embodied in mechanisms, but they did not think.
4. The bridge to computing is not magic. It is the gradual separation of
   symbol, rule, mechanism, and human judgment.

## Scope

In scope:

- human calculation before electronic computers
- counting boards, abaci, rods, tables, and mechanical calculators as aids to
  human work
- selected automata and astronomical mechanisms as evidence that people could
  imagine and build self-moving mechanisms
- symbolic procedures and the idea that reasoning might be made calculable
- analogy limits for general readers
- technical companion notes for compute, symbol, procedure, mechanism,
  automation, and simulation

Out of scope:

- a complete world history of mathematics
- claims that ancient or early modern automata were AI
- claims that every calculation aid is a direct ancestor of modern computers
- private anecdotes, client examples, proprietary project histories, or
  personal information
- unsupported origin claims such as "the first computer" without qualification

## Article Shape

### 1. Start With A Human Problem

Open with a recognizable problem: numbers become too many, too repetitive, or
too consequential to trust to memory alone. Navigation, astronomy, trade,
taxation, engineering, and later scientific work all needed reliable results.
The article should begin with that need, not with a machine.

### 2. Calculation Was Work Before It Was Hardware

Explain that "computer" was once an occupation. Human computers performed
calculation as organized work, sometimes in teams, sometimes using tables,
forms, slide rules, or desk machines. The social point matters: this labor was
often clerical, gendered, under-credited, and tied to institutions that needed
scale.

### 3. Tools Externalized Fragile Steps

Use counting boards, abaci, Napier's rods, logarithmic tables, and similar aids
to show the pattern: the tool does not replace judgment. It lets a person move
part of the calculation into a visible structure that can be checked, taught,
and repeated.

### 4. Mechanisms Embodied Operations

Introduce mechanical calculators as a shift from hand-aided arithmetic toward
machines that embody specific operations. Pascal's and Leibniz's machines can
help explain carries, repeated addition, and gear-based arithmetic. The article
should keep the boundary clear: these are calculating mechanisms, not general
intelligence.

### 5. Automata Made Mechanism Feel Alive

Use automata carefully. Clockwork figures, musical machines, and mechanical
animals show that people could build devices that appeared to act on their own.
They also show how easily audiences project agency onto mechanism. The
Mechanical Turk is useful only as a cautionary example about hidden human
labor and spectacle, not as a true AI milestone.

### 6. Symbols And Procedures Became Thinkable As Machinery

Connect procedural arithmetic, algebraic methods, and Leibniz's dream of
calculable reasoning to the next article's formal-logic lane. This section
should be short. Its job is to open the door to formal logic and computation,
not to cover that full history.

### 7. End At The Threshold

Close with Babbage and the broader nineteenth-century shift: machines could be
imagined not only as tools for one operation, but as systems that used
instructions, memory-like stores, and controlled sequences. The ending should
point forward without making AI feel inevitable.

## Core Claims And Boundaries

| Claim | Use | Boundary |
|---|---|---|
| Human computers performed calculation before electronic machines. | Establish that computing begins as work, not hardware. | Do not erase labor conditions, gender, institutions, or checking practices. |
| Abaci and counting boards externalized arithmetic into manipulable state. | Show how tools make number work visible and teachable. | The human operator still supplies the rule, control, and interpretation. |
| Tables and rods reduced work by reusing precomputed structure. | Introduce lookup and decomposition without modern jargon. | Do not call a printed table a database or a rod a program except as a limited analogy. |
| Mechanical calculators embodied arithmetic operations in gears and wheels. | Show the move from aided calculation to automatic operation. | Fixed-purpose calculators are not general-purpose computers or AI systems. |
| Automata made mechanism look like self-directed action. | Explain cultural imagination around artificial life and agency. | Apparent agency is not cognition, learning, or understanding. |
| Jacquard cards controlled textile patterns and influenced later input/control ideas. | Bridge pattern storage and machine control. | The loom did not compute in the modern general-purpose sense. |
| Babbage's engines mark a conceptual threshold for automatic calculation. | End the article by pointing to programmable computation. | Babbage belongs at the threshold, not as proof that AI had arrived. |
| Leibniz imagined reasoning as a kind of calculation. | Foreshadow formal logic. | Treat as intellectual background, not a straight causal line to modern AI. |

Full claim-to-source mappings are in `source-map.yaml`.

## Analogy Rules

Use analogies as teaching devices, not evidence.

Recommended analogies:

- Abacus as working memory: helps show a visible calculation state. Limit:
  the person supplies the algorithm.
- Mathematical table as cached work: helps show precomputed reuse. Limit:
  the table is static and narrow.
- Napier's rods as lookup plus transformation: helps show decomposition.
  Limit: the rods do not decide what to do next.
- Jacquard cards as stored pattern instructions: helps show controlled
  sequence. Limit: weaving instructions are not general symbolic programs.
- Automata as theater of mechanism: helps show projected agency. Limit:
  fixed motion is not perception, learning, or reasoning.
- Human computer office as distributed calculation: helps show scale. Limit:
  people are not interchangeable processors, and labor conditions matter.

Avoid these analogies:

- "Ancient AI"
- "The abacus was the first computer" without careful qualification
- "The Antikythera mechanism predicted like machine learning"
- "Automata were robots in the modern AI sense"
- "Babbage directly caused modern AI"

## Technical Companion Notes

Use the companion to define terms that would slow the main story:

- `compute`: to determine a result by following a procedure. Historically,
  this could be human work before it became machine work.
- `symbol`: a mark, position, bead, digit, or notation that stands for
  something else.
- `procedure`: an ordered method that can be taught, repeated, checked, and
  sometimes delegated.
- `mechanism`: a physical arrangement that constrains motion so one action
  reliably produces another.
- `automation`: execution of a process with reduced moment-to-moment human
  intervention.
- `simulation`: a model that imitates selected behavior of a system. Simulation
  is not the same as understanding.
- `program`: defer the full definition to the later formal-computation article;
  here, use only as a boundary term.
